Our Story

The bridge, not the claim

Why Mahjong Mazaa exists, what we stand for,
and how we're building a cultural bridge through play.

"We're not claiming mahjong. We're hosting it."

Mahjong Mazaa was born from a simple realization: mahjong is a game that knows how to find its way home, no matter whose table it lands on. From Chinese teahouses to Jewish living rooms to South Asian gatherings—this game has always been about community, strategy, and the ritual of coming together.

What Mahjong Mazaa Is

A Cultural Bridge

Mahjong Mazaa is a tile set designed with South Asian aesthetics—warm colors, culturally resonant patterns, and an invitation to gather. But we're not reinventing the game. The tiles are still Dots, Bams, and Cracks. The rules are still the rules. The strategy is still the strategy.

What's different is the feeling. These tiles feel at home on South Asian tables. They belong alongside chai or coffee, amidst multigenerational conversations, in spaces where hospitality isn't just offered—it's woven into the fabric of gathering.

We're not claiming mahjong as "ours." We're creating tiles that let South Asian communities feel seen, welcomed, and invited to participate in a tradition that has always crossed cultural boundaries.

What Mahjong Mazaa Is Not

Clarity matters

Not Appropriation

We're not taking mahjong, renaming it, and pretending it's South Asian. We honor its Chinese origins explicitly. We name its complicated American history honestly. And we position ourselves as hosts, not owners.

Not a Reinvention

We're not "modernizing" or "improving" mahjong. This game doesn't need fixing. It's been played and beloved for over 150 years. We're simply offering an aesthetic that feels resonant to a new community.

Not Erasure

We don't skip over the racism of the 1920s American fad. We don't gloss over Orientalist marketing. We don't minimize the identity-building work of Jewish women in the 1930s-50s. All of this matters.

Not Exclusive

These tiles aren't "only for South Asians." They're for anyone who appreciates the aesthetic, the cultural care, and the invitation to gather. Mahjong has always been a game that welcomes.

The History We Honor

A game that crosses cultures and centuries

From Chinese teahouses to American living rooms to tables around the world—
this is the story of how mahjong became a global phenomenon.

Mahjong's journey is not simple. It's a story of cultural exchange, appropriation, community-building, and adaptation. Understanding this history is essential to understanding why Mahjong Mazaa exists—and how we position ourselves as a bridge, not a claim.

We don't erase the problematic moments. We honor the full story.

Mid-1800s

Origins in China

Mahjong emerges in Southern China toward the end of the Qing Dynasty (1644–1912). It likely evolved from earlier card games like madiao. The game first appeared in print in 19th-century Chinese literature.

Initially a pastime for Chinese aristocrats, it gradually spread to the general public. The name "mahjong" (麻將/麻雀) means "sparrow" in southern Chinese dialects, referring to the bird-like clacking sound the tiles make during reshuffling.

1920

American Arrival & The Complicated Fad

American businessman Joseph Park Babcock, living in Shanghai, brought mahjong to the United States. He simplified the rules and published "Rules of Mah-Jongg," making it more accessible to Western audiences.

The game became an enormous national fad, particularly among wealthy white women. However, the story was complicated by racism and Orientalism. Marketers falsely claimed mahjong was thousands of years old, played by "ancient Chinese court" members.

This is the tension Mahjong Mazaa acknowledges: a beautiful game marred by racist marketing and appropriation.

1920s-1930s

Chinese Americans & Cultural Bonding

While white Americans played mahjong as an exotic fad, Chinese Americans used the game for authentic cultural connection. Mahjong became central to cultural bonding in Chinatowns across America, particularly in Manhattan.

The game served as a powerful link to homeland traditions and helped maintain community identity during a period of intense discrimination and exclusion.

1937

Jewish Women & The National Mah Jongg League

Jewish women—many of them children of Eastern European immigrants—formed the National Mah Jongg League (NMJL). They standardized American Mahjong with unique rules including Jokers, the "Charleston" (tile passing), and an annual card of approved hands.

This wasn't just about gameplay. For Jewish women experiencing newfound upward mobility and suburban assimilation after World War II, mahjong became an identity marker—a way to maintain cultural connection while navigating whiteness and belonging.

This history matters to Mahjong Mazaa because it shows how marginalized communities have used mahjong to create belonging—something we aim to continue, not appropriate.

1940s-1950s

African American Women & Community Building

Mahjong also served as a crucial community-building exercise for African American clubwomen in the urban North and Midwest. These groups used the game to create social networks and intellectual gathering spaces during a period of limited public access.

Additionally, incarcerated Japanese Americans during World War II played mahjong as a way to maintain cultural connection and community during internment.

1960s-1980s

Solidification & Global Spread

While the fad among white Americans evaporated by the end of the "roaring twenties," the game remained a beloved pastime in Chinese and Jewish families. It also spread across Asia with unique variations: Riichi Mahjong in Japan, and distinct styles in Vietnam, Singapore, Malaysia, and the Philippines.

There are now 40+ variants of mahjong worldwide, with 30+ in mainland China alone. This adaptability is by design—the game belongs to the communities who play it.

Today

A Global Gathering

Mahjong remains beloved in Chinese families worldwide, passed down through generations as a link to cultural heritage. It continues to be a defining game for Jewish-American communities, particularly in suburban areas and retirement communities.

Now, Mahjong Mazaa enters the story. We're not the first to adapt mahjong, and we won't be the last. But we enter with transparency: acknowledging the Chinese origins, respecting the Jewish-American evolution, being honest about the racism of the 1920s fad, and welcoming South Asian communities through culturally resonant design.

The game continues its journey. We're honored to host it.

What This History Teaches Us

Complexity Matters

Mahjong's history is not simple. It includes beauty and racism, community-building and appropriation, tradition and evolution. We don't get to pick and choose—we honor it all.

Marginalized Communities Found Belonging

Chinese immigrants, Jewish women, African American clubwomen, and incarcerated Japanese Americans all used mahjong to create community during difficult times. This pattern of gathering is what we continue.

Mahjong adapted wherever it landed. That's not erasure—it's the nature of cultural exchange when done with respect. Our job is to make sure that respect is always present.

How to Play Mahjong

A game of skill, strategy, and luck

Whether you're completely new or brushing up on the rules,
this guide will help you start playing.

The Basics

Mahjong is a four-player game that blends skill and chance, similar to gin rummy or poker. The goal is to build a complete hand of 14 tiles by drawing and discarding tiles throughout the game.

Players

4 players (though variants exist for 2-3 players)

Tiles

144 tiles total in standard set

Duration

30-60 minutes per game

Objective

Build a winning hand of 14 tiles

The Tiles

Understanding what you're playing with

Suits (108 tiles)

  • Bamboos: numbered 1-9 (36 tiles)
  • Characters: numbered 1-9 (36 tiles)
  • Dots/Circles: numbered 1-9 (36 tiles)

Honor Tiles (28 tiles)

  • Winds: East, South, West, North (16 tiles)
  • Dragons: Red, Green, White (12 tiles)

Bonus Tiles (8 tiles)

  • Flowers: Plum, Orchid, Chrysanthemum, Bamboo
  • Seasons: Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter

Basic Gameplay

1. Setup & Deal

Build walls with all tiles face-down. Each player receives 13 tiles. The dealer starts with 14 tiles.

2. Draw & Discard

On your turn, draw one tile from the wall. If you don't need it, discard one tile face-up to the center. The goal is to organize your hand into sets.

3. Build Sets

Create combinations:

  • Pong: Three identical tiles
  • Kong: Four identical tiles
  • Chow: Three consecutive tiles of the same suit

4. Winning Hand

A complete winning hand usually consists of four sets plus one pair (14 tiles total). Declare "Mahjong!" when you complete your hand.

5. Scoring

Points are awarded based on the hand's composition and difficulty. Different variants have different scoring systems.

Choosing Your Variant

There are many ways to play mahjong

Classical/Chinese Mahjong

The original version from China. More flexible rules, various regional differences. Played widely across Asia.

  • Standard 144 tiles
  • Flexible hand patterns
  • Complex scoring system
  • Regional variations common

American Mahjong

Created by the National Mah Jongg League in 1937. Uses an annual card with approved hand patterns.

  • Includes Joker tiles (wild cards)
  • "Charleston" (tile passing ritual)
  • Annual card with approved hands
  • Popular in Jewish-American communities
