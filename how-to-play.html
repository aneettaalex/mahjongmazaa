How to Play Mahjong

A game of skill, strategy, and luck

Whether you're completely new or brushing up on the rules,
this guide will help you start playing.

The Basics

Mahjong is a four-player game that blends skill and chance, similar to gin rummy or poker. The goal is to build a complete hand of 14 tiles by drawing and discarding tiles throughout the game.

Players

4 players (though variants exist for 2-3 players)

Tiles

144 tiles total in standard set

Duration

30-60 minutes per game

Objective

Build a winning hand of 14 tiles

The Tiles

Understanding what you're playing with

Suits (108 tiles)

  • Bamboos: numbered 1-9 (36 tiles)
  • Characters: numbered 1-9 (36 tiles)
  • Dots/Circles: numbered 1-9 (36 tiles)

Honor Tiles (28 tiles)

  • Winds: East, South, West, North (16 tiles)
  • Dragons: Red, Green, White (12 tiles)

Bonus Tiles (8 tiles)

  • Flowers: Plum, Orchid, Chrysanthemum, Bamboo
  • Seasons: Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter

Basic Gameplay

1. Setup & Deal

Build walls with all tiles face-down. Each player receives 13 tiles. The dealer starts with 14 tiles.

2. Draw & Discard

On your turn, draw one tile from the wall. If you don't need it, discard one tile face-up to the center. The goal is to organize your hand into sets.

3. Build Sets

Create combinations:

  • Pong: Three identical tiles
  • Kong: Four identical tiles
  • Chow: Three consecutive tiles of the same suit

4. Winning Hand

A complete winning hand usually consists of four sets plus one pair (14 tiles total). Declare "Mahjong!" when you complete your hand.

5. Scoring

Points are awarded based on the hand's composition and difficulty. Different variants have different scoring systems.

Choosing Your Variant

There are many ways to play mahjong

Classical/Chinese Mahjong

The original version from China. More flexible rules, various regional differences. Played widely across Asia.

  • Standard 144 tiles
  • Flexible hand patterns
  • Complex scoring system
  • Regional variations common

American Mahjong

Created by the National Mah Jongg League in 1937. Uses an annual card with approved hand patterns.

  • Includes Joker tiles (wild cards)
  • "Charleston" (tile passing ritual)
  • Annual card with approved hands
  • Popular in Jewish-American communities
